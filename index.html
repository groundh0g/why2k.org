... a social media experiment.

by @groundh0g

I'm working on two* book projects. Instead of doing something like a kickstarter campaign, I thought I would try something different ... Rather than voting with their dollars, interested parties could part with something a bit less valuable — their Twitter follows and Facebook likes.

* Technically, I'm working on three books if you count my stalled novel.
* Download the 17 or so sample chapters if you need an effective, drug-free sleep aid.

How Does It Work?

The project with the most "votes" will get the lion's share of my scarce and precious free time. At the time of this writing, my Twitter following is 445 and my Facebook following is 271, so my game development book (in theory) should be getting roughly 60% of the hours away from work and family, with the balance funneled into my pun-a-day project.

Meh. I Need More Than That!

OK. If either project gets the magical 2,000 number of followers, I'll opensource it under the MIT license. The LeanPub markdown for each book will be hosted on GitHub, and the resulting PDF will be available as a free download (without DRM).

You've Gotten My Attention. But I Still Need More.

Good Lord. It's just a freakin' like or follow. But, if you insist ...

I'll write the books in the open. The entire process will be transparent, and you can offer your feedback throughout the projects' development. As a part of that workflow, I'll make regular updates to the "published" PDFs. And, of course, those will be available as free downloads. No email farming, no ads, no gimmicks.

So, What Do You Get Out Of It?

I get a little direction. I get a little motivation. Plus, I don't have to work in a vacuum.

This is a simple and free way for me to generate a little buzz around the projects, which I hope will get my work into the hands of more readers. It's also an accountability tool for me. If I know there are thousands of people expecting me to finish a project, I'm more likely to finish it.

OK. So, Tell Me About The Projects.

Wow. You're still reading? I expected a lot more TLDR responses.

Sure! I'll give an overview of each project here. If you'd like more detailed information, ping me on Twitter or Facebook. Maybe I can write blog posts with plans, roadmaps, updates, and other big-boy stuff.

Book 1 — MonoGame: Code Camps

In August of 2007, my game programming book (XNA Game Studio Express: Developing Games for Windows and the Xbox 360) was published. It was fairly well received.

Since 2011, I've been teaching a game programming class for teens and tweens every June in North Carolina. The class has been based on my XNA book, but XNA was mothballed in 2014. So, I've moved over to, and started contributing to, the open source equivalent — MonoGame.

New technology? New book. This time around, I'm basing the example games on the games that my students have created over the years, with a few more that interest me thrown in.

Book 2 — Fauxcabulary

In August of 2010, at the urging of my wife, I started a little project (Fauxcabulary.org) to capture my countless bad puns. I now have enough daily puns queued up to carry me through most of 2017.

I originally intended the project to become a series of word-a-day calendars. Those hopes were squashed when I realized the cost and logistics of stocking and shipping the little guys would bankrupt me.

The new plan is to make a dictionary-like tome. You can download a sample here to see what I'm thinking.

I'm Hooked. How Do We Light A Fire Under Your Fanny?

Vote! It's just that easy.

  • For my MonoGame book, follow me on Twitter.
  • For my Fauxcabulary book, follow me on Facebook.

If you'd like to help generate buzz for these projects, use the "#why2k" hashtag in your tweets or posts about the Why2K.org campaign. Be sure to mention which book you're pimping. Some good hashtags to include are "#monogame #gamedev" or "#fauxcabulary #amreading".

And remember that there's nothing keeping you from voting for both projects. =)

No. I'm Really Hooked. How Can I Help?

Read. Criticize. Repeat.

If the project goes open source, you can contribute directly to the GitHub repositories via pull requests. If you don't know what that means, we'll figure out some other way to get editing into the hands of the readers.

For Fauxcabulary, I'm looking for word nerds. Avid readers. Linguaphiles. Folks with a sense of humor who aren't afraid to say, "I don't get it". I may also need people to temper my political incorrectness.

For MonoGame: Code Camps, I'm looking for game programming hobbyist and enthusiasts to do technical reviews and sanity checks. I need folks who aren't afraid of an IDE and compiler. You don't have to be a John Carmack, but some programming experience (any platform, any language) would be nice — even if it's not in game development.
